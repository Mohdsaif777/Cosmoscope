Aryabhata (1975)
Objective: India's first satellite aimed to test various space technologies. Read more...

Launched by the Soviet Union, Aryabhata marked India's entry into space research. The satellite conducted experiments in X-ray astronomy and solar physics, though technical issues led to power loss shortly after reaching orbit. Despite these setbacks, it was pivotal in gaining expertise in satellite design and systems, setting a foundation for India’s space technology.

Luna Program (1959-1976)
Objective: Conduct lunar exploration with unmanned probes. Read more...

Luna missions achieved several space exploration firsts, including the first impact on the Moon (Luna 2, 1959) and the first photos of the Moon’s far side (Luna 3, 1959). Luna 9 (1966) was the first to perform a soft landing on the Moon, sending back panoramic images of the surface, while Luna 16, 20, and 24 successfully returned lunar soil samples to Earth. These missions provided invaluable data on the lunar surface, composition, and temperature, advancing lunar science.

Venera Program (1961-1984)
Objective: Explore Venus and study its atmosphere and surface. Read more...

The Venera program achieved the first human-made impact on Venus (Venera 3, 1966), the first landing on another planet (Venera 7, 1970), and the first images of Venus’ surface (Venera 9, 1975). Later missions (Venera 13 and 14, 1982) conducted chemical analyses of Venusian soil and provided data on its atmosphere, which is thick with sulfuric acid clouds. Venera's data helped shape our understanding of Venus’ extreme conditions, high atmospheric pressure, and surface composition.

Soyuz Program (1967-Present)
Objective: Human spaceflight missions, including crew transport and cargo. Read more...

Soyuz has become the world’s most reliable crewed spacecraft, instrumental in space station support, including transporting astronauts to the International Space Station (ISS) since 2000. Early missions provided essential experience for long-term human spaceflight, while later missions continuously advanced life-support, safety, and re-entry technology. Soyuz is integral to international space collaboration and continues to support ISS operations.

Salyut Space Station Program (1971-1986)
Objective: Develop space station technology and conduct long-duration human spaceflight experiments. Read more...

The Salyut program included the world’s first space station, Salyut 1, which laid the groundwork for extended human missions. Later stations, like Salyut 6 and Salyut 7, supported scientific experiments on Earth observation, materials science, and medicine, paving the way for future space station designs, particularly the Mir space station. The program’s successes and challenges, including crewed docking and repairs, set standards for orbital operations.

Objective: Study Mars with orbiters and landers. Read more...

Mars 3 was the first spacecraft to land on Mars (1971), although it ceased transmission shortly after landing. Mars orbiters like Mars 5 sent back data on the Martian atmosphere and surface. While facing technical difficulties, the missions contributed to early mapping efforts and increased knowledge of Martian conditions, which helped inform later international Mars missions.

Mir Space Station (1986-2001)
Objective: Long-term human habitation in space and international collaboration. Read more...

Mir was the world’s first modular space station, supporting complex scientific and biological research in orbit for nearly 15 years. It hosted international astronauts and became a hub for joint space missions with the U.S. and European Space Agencies. Mir allowed for unprecedented research into the effects of long-term space exposure on the human body and refined techniques for on-orbit repairs, laying the groundwork for the ISS.
